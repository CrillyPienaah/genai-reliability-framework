Child-Pugh Score — Liver Disease Severity Assessment

DEFINITION AND PURPOSE:
The Child-Pugh score (also Child-Turcotte-Pugh score) is a clinical scoring system used to assess the severity of chronic liver disease, particularly cirrhosis. It is used to predict prognosis, surgical risk, and guide treatment decisions including liver transplant listing.

PARAMETERS (each scored 1-3):
1. Total bilirubin (mg/dL): < 2 (1 pt), 2-3 (2 pts), > 3 (3 pts)
2. Serum albumin (g/dL): > 3.5 (1 pt), 2.8-3.5 (2 pts), < 2.8 (3 pts)
3. Prothrombin time / INR: INR < 1.7 (1 pt), 1.7-2.3 (2 pts), > 2.3 (3 pts)
4. Ascites: absent (1 pt), mild/controlled (2 pts), moderate-severe/refractory (3 pts)
5. Hepatic encephalopathy: none (1 pt), grade 1-2 (2 pts), grade 3-4 (3 pts)

CLASSIFICATION:
Child-Pugh Class A: 5-6 points — well-compensated cirrhosis — 1-year survival ~100%, 2-year ~85%
Child-Pugh Class B: 7-9 points — significant functional compromise — 1-year survival ~80%, 2-year ~60%
Child-Pugh Class C: 10-15 points — decompensated cirrhosis — 1-year survival ~45%, 2-year ~35%

CLINICAL APPLICATIONS:
- Surgical risk stratification: Class A — low risk; Class B — moderate risk; Class C — high operative mortality (generally contraindicates elective surgery)
- Transplant listing criteria (used alongside MELD score)
- Drug dosing adjustments in hepatic impairment
- Prognosis discussion with patients

LIMITATIONS:
Some components (ascites, encephalopathy) are subjective. The MELD score (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease) is now preferred for transplant prioritisation due to better predictive validity.
