Metformin — Mechanism of Action and Clinical Pharmacology

DRUG CLASS: Biguanide antihyperglycaemic agent

PRIMARY MECHANISM OF ACTION:
Metformin primarily acts by activating AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) in hepatic tissue. AMPK activation suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis — the production of new glucose by the liver from non-carbohydrate precursors (lactate, amino acids, glycerol). Hepatic gluconeogenesis is the main driver of fasting hyperglycaemia in Type 2 diabetes mellitus.

Additional mechanisms:
- Improves peripheral insulin sensitivity in skeletal muscle, increasing glucose uptake
- Reduces intestinal glucose absorption (minor contribution)
- May alter gut microbiome and incretin hormone secretion

MOLECULAR TARGET:
Metformin inhibits Complex I of the mitochondrial electron transport chain, reducing ATP production and raising the AMP:ATP ratio, which activates AMPK. It also activates AMPK via AMPK kinase (LKB1)-dependent pathways.

ADVANTAGES OVER OTHER ANTIDIABETIC AGENTS:
- Does NOT stimulate insulin secretion — minimal hypoglycaemia risk as monotherapy (unlike sulfonylureas)
- Weight-neutral or promotes modest weight loss (unlike insulin, sulfonylureas, thiazolidinediones)
- No risk of hypoglycaemia when used alone
- Cardiovascular outcome benefit demonstrated in UKPDS (United Kingdom Prospective Diabetes Study) in overweight T2DM patients
- Inexpensive, widely available, oral administration
- Long safety record (>60 years clinical use)

LACTIC ACIDOSIS:
Rare but serious adverse effect. Risk increases with renal impairment, hepatic failure, excessive alcohol use, conditions causing tissue hypoxia. Contraindicated in eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m².

GASTROINTESTINAL SIDE EFFECTS:
Nausea, diarrhoea, abdominal discomfort in up to 30% of patients — usually transient. Minimised by starting at low dose and titrating slowly, taking with food.
