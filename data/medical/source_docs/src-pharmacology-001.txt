Beta-Adrenergic Blockers (Beta-Blockers) — Pharmacology Reference

MECHANISM OF ACTION:
Beta-blockers competitively antagonize catecholamines (epinephrine and norepinephrine) at beta-adrenergic receptors. They bind beta receptors reversibly and dose-dependently.

RECEPTOR SELECTIVITY:
Beta-1 receptors (cardiac): located primarily in the heart and kidneys.
Beta-2 receptors: located in bronchial smooth muscle, vascular smooth muscle, uterus, liver.

Beta-1 selective (cardioselective) agents: metoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol, nebivolol.
Non-selective agents (block both beta-1 and beta-2): propranolol, carvedilol, labetalol.

CARDIAC EFFECTS (Beta-1 blockade):
- Reduces heart rate (negative chronotropy) by decreasing SA node automaticity
- Reduces myocardial contractility (negative inotropy) — decreases cardiac output
- Slows AV conduction velocity (negative dromotropy) — useful for rate control
- Reduces blood pressure by lowering cardiac output and renin release

PULMONARY EFFECTS (Beta-2 blockade):
Non-selective beta-blockers cause bronchoconstriction — contraindicated in asthma and severe COPD.

METABOLIC EFFECTS:
Can mask tachycardia (a key warning sign of hypoglycaemia) — use with caution in insulin-dependent diabetes.
Non-selective agents impair glycogenolysis and may prolong hypoglycaemia.

CLINICAL USES:
Hypertension, heart failure (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol — with dose titration), angina pectoris, post-myocardial infarction, cardiac arrhythmias (rate control in atrial fibrillation), hyperthyroidism (symptom control), migraine prophylaxis, essential tremor, portal hypertension.

CONTRAINDICATIONS:
Cardiogenic shock, decompensated heart failure, severe bradycardia, high-degree AV block without pacemaker, asthma (non-selective agents).
