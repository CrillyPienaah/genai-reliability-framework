Colorectal Cancer Screening — Clinical Guidelines

RECOMMENDATION:
Average-risk adults should begin colorectal cancer (CRC) screening at age 45, per the American Cancer Society (2018) and US Preventive Services Task Force (2021) guidelines. Screening should continue to age 75 for average-risk individuals.

HIGH-RISK PATIENTS (earlier/more frequent screening):
- First-degree relative with CRC or advanced polyp before age 60: begin at age 40 or 10 years before youngest affected relative
- Lynch syndrome or familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP): specialised protocols
- Inflammatory bowel disease: colonoscopy every 1-2 years after 8 years of disease

SCREENING OPTIONS FOR AVERAGE-RISK ADULTS:

STRUCTURAL EXAMS (visualise colon directly):
- Colonoscopy: every 10 years — gold standard, allows biopsy and polypectomy
- CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy): every 5 years
- Flexible sigmoidoscopy: every 5 years (examines left colon only)

STOOL-BASED TESTS:
- High-sensitivity guaiac fecal occult blood test (gFOBT): annually
- Fecal immunochemical test (FIT): annually — preferred over gFOBT
- Stool DNA test (Cologuard — FIT-DNA): every 1-3 years

FOLLOW-UP:
A positive result on any non-invasive stool-based test requires follow-up diagnostic colonoscopy. An abnormal stool test followed by a negative colonoscopy means the patient should return to routine screening, not repeat stool testing.
