Pulmonary Embolism — Diagnosis and Clinical Presentation

DEFINITION:
Pulmonary embolism (PE) is obstruction of the pulmonary arterial system, most commonly by thrombus originating from deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the lower extremities.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
PE presentation varies widely from asymptomatic to sudden death.

Classic symptoms:
- Sudden onset dyspnoea (most common symptom, present in ~80% of cases)
- Pleuritic chest pain (sharp, worsens with breathing — suggests pulmonary infarction)
- Haemoptysis (coughing blood — less common, suggests infarction)
- Tachycardia (heart rate > 100 bpm — common, non-specific)

Massive PE symptoms:
- Hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg)
- Syncope or presyncope
- Right heart strain

Many PEs are clinically silent and found incidentally on imaging.

RISK FACTORS (Virchow's Triad):
Stasis: prolonged immobilisation, long-haul travel, hospitalisation, heart failure
Hypercoagulability: malignancy, pregnancy, oral contraceptives, thrombophilia, antiphospholipid syndrome
Endothelial injury: recent surgery, trauma, central venous catheter

DIAGNOSIS:
Clinical probability: Wells criteria score (pre-test probability assessment)
D-dimer: high sensitivity, low specificity — useful to exclude PE in low/moderate probability
CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA): gold standard for diagnosis
Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scan: alternative when CTPA contraindicated
Echocardiography: for haemodynamically unstable patients

TREATMENT:
Anticoagulation: heparin (UFH or LMWH) followed by warfarin or direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs — apixaban, rivaroxaban preferred).
Thrombolysis: for massive PE with haemodynamic instability.
Embolectomy: surgical or catheter-directed for selected cases.
