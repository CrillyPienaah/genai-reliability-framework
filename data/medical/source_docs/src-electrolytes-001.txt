Hyperkalaemia — Clinical Management

DEFINITION AND CLASSIFICATION:
Normal serum potassium: 3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L
Mild hyperkalaemia: 5.1 to 5.9 mEq/L
Moderate hyperkalaemia: 6.0 to 6.4 mEq/L
Severe hyperkalaemia: ≥ 6.5 mEq/L or any level with ECG changes

A potassium of 5.8 mEq/L represents mild to moderate hyperkalaemia. Risk of cardiac arrhythmias increases significantly above 6.0 mEq/L.

ECG CHANGES (in order of increasing severity):
1. Peaked (tall, narrow, symmetric) T waves — earliest change
2. Prolonged PR interval
3. Widened QRS complex
4. Sine wave pattern
5. Ventricular fibrillation / asystole

CAUSES:
Renal insufficiency (most common), ACE inhibitor or ARB use, potassium-sparing diuretics, hypoaldosteronism, acidosis, haemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, excessive potassium intake.

TREATMENT:
Calcium gluconate (10 mL of 10% solution IV): stabilises cardiac membrane — used when ECG changes present. Does NOT lower potassium. Onset within minutes.

Insulin (10 units regular) + Glucose (25-50g dextrose): shifts potassium into cells. Onset 20-30 minutes, lowers K+ by 0.5-1.0 mEq/L.

Sodium bicarbonate: in acidotic patients, shifts K+ intracellularly. Less effective in non-acidotic patients.

Salbutamol (albuterol) nebulised: shifts K+ intracellularly. Lowers K+ by 0.5-1.5 mEq/L.

Cation exchange resins (patiromer, sodium zirconium cyclosilicate): remove potassium from GI tract. Onset hours to days — not for acute management.

Dietary restriction: limit high-potassium foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes, tomatoes).

Dialysis: for severe, refractory hyperkalaemia or patients with end-stage renal disease.
