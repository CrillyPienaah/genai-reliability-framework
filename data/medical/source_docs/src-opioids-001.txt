Opioid Safety and Respiratory Depression

OPIOID-INDUCED RESPIRATORY DEPRESSION:
Respiratory depression is the most dangerous adverse effect of opioid medications. Opioids act on mu-opioid receptors in the brainstem respiratory centres, reducing respiratory rate and tidal volume.

RECOGNITION:
Warning signs of opioid-induced respiratory depression:
- Respiratory rate < 12 breaths per minute (bradypnoea)
- Respiratory rate of 8 breaths per minute indicates significant respiratory depression
- Decreased oxygen saturation (SpO2 < 90%)
- Constricted (pinpoint) pupils — miosis
- Decreased level of consciousness / sedation
- Cyanosis (late sign)

A respiratory rate of 8 breaths per minute is near the threshold for respiratory arrest, particularly in opioid-naive patients or those who have received recent doses. This requires immediate assessment.

MORPHINE AND RESPIRATORY DEPRESSION:
Morphine must NOT be administered to a patient with a respiratory rate of 8 breaths per minute or significant respiratory depression. Administering additional opioids in this setting can precipitate respiratory arrest.

OPIOID OVERDOSE TRIAD:
1. Miosis (pinpoint pupils)
2. Unconsciousness / decreased level of consciousness
3. Respiratory depression (rate < 12/min)

NALOXONE (NARCAN) — OPIOID ANTAGONIST:
Naloxone reverses opioid-induced respiratory depression by competitively blocking mu-opioid receptors.

Dose: 0.4 to 2 mg IV/IM/SC or intranasal. May repeat every 2-3 minutes up to 10 mg. Use lower doses (0.04-0.1 mg) in opioid-dependent patients to avoid precipitating acute withdrawal.

Onset: IV — within 2 minutes. Duration: 30-90 minutes (shorter than most opioids — repeat dosing or infusion may be required).

AIRWAY:
Ensure airway protection. Bag-mask ventilation if respiratory rate critically low. Prepare for potential intubation if no response to naloxone.
