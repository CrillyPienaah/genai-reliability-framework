Type 1 vs Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus — Comparison

TYPE 1 DIABETES MELLITUS (T1DM):
Pathophysiology: Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells leading to absolute insulin deficiency. T-cell mediated immune attack destroys the insulin-producing cells of the islets of Langerhans.

Typical onset: Childhood, adolescence, or young adulthood (though can present at any age)
Body habitus: Usually lean/normal weight at diagnosis
C-peptide: Low or undetectable (reflects absent beta cell function)
Autoantibodies: Positive in 90%+ (GAD65, IA-2, insulin autoantibodies, ZnT8)
DKA risk: High — patients are prone to diabetic ketoacidosis without insulin

Treatment: Requires insulin therapy — always. Multiple daily injections or continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion (insulin pump). Intensive glucose monitoring. No oral agents are effective as primary treatment.

TYPE 2 DIABETES MELLITUS (T2DM):
Pathophysiology: Combination of insulin resistance (peripheral tissues fail to respond normally to insulin) and relative insulin deficiency (beta cells cannot produce enough insulin to overcome resistance). Beta cells are not destroyed.

Typical onset: Middle-age to older adults (though increasingly common in younger people with obesity)
Risk factors: Obesity, physical inactivity, family history, ethnicity (South Asian, Indigenous, Hispanic), gestational diabetes history
Body habitus: Strongly associated with overweight and obesity (BMI > 25-30 kg/m²)
C-peptide: Normal or elevated initially (compensatory hyperinsulinaemia)

Treatment: Lifestyle modification (diet, exercise, weight loss) + metformin first-line. Oral agents, non-insulin injectables (GLP-1 agonists), and eventually insulin when required. HbA1c, fasting glucose, and clinical context distinguish T1DM from T2DM. LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of Adults) can mimic T2DM initially.
