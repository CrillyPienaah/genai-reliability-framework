Cardiac Biomarkers — Troponin in Acute Coronary Syndromes

CARDIAC TROPONINS:
Troponin I (TnI) and Troponin T (TnT) are structural proteins of the cardiac myocyte contractile apparatus. They are released into the bloodstream when myocardial cell injury or death occurs.

Cardiac troponin is the preferred biomarker for diagnosis of myocardial infarction (MI).

HIGH-SENSITIVITY TROPONIN ASSAYS (hs-cTn):
High-sensitivity troponin assays detect myocardial injury earlier and at lower concentrations. Serial measurements at 0 and 1-2 hours (or 0 and 3 hours) with high-sensitivity assays are the standard diagnostic approach.

INTERPRETATION IN CHEST PAIN:
In the context of ischaemic symptoms (chest pain, dyspnoea, jaw pain, left arm pain), elevated troponin above the 99th percentile URL (upper reference limit) with a dynamic rise or fall supports the diagnosis of acute myocardial infarction (AMI).

NSTEMI (Non-ST elevation MI): elevated troponin without ST elevation on ECG
STEMI (ST elevation MI): elevated troponin with ST elevation — requires emergent reperfusion

CLINICAL CORRELATION IS ESSENTIAL:
Troponin can be elevated in non-ischaemic conditions including:
- Myocarditis (inflammation of myocardium)
- Pulmonary embolism (right heart strain)
- Decompensated heart failure
- Renal failure (impaired clearance)
- Sepsis and critical illness
- Cardiac contusion
- Cardioversion or ablation procedures
- Rhabdomyolysis

A rising and falling pattern (dynamic change) is more specific for AMI than a single elevated value. ECG interpretation, clinical history, and risk stratification are essential components of evaluation alongside troponin.
