Acetaminophen Toxicology and Overdose Management

EPIDEMIOLOGY:
Acetaminophen overdose is the leading cause of acute liver failure in North America and the United Kingdom. It accounts for approximately 50% of all acute liver failure cases.

TOXIC DOSES:
Hepatotoxicity can occur at doses above 7.5 to 10 grams in healthy adults. Severe hepatotoxicity is common above 150 mg/kg or 7.5-10g total. In chronic alcohol users, malnourished individuals, or those on CYP2E1 inducers, toxicity may occur at lower doses.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
At toxic doses, glucuronidation and sulfation pathways are saturated. CYP2E1 produces excess NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine). Glutathione stores are depleted, allowing NAPQI to bind hepatic proteins causing centrilobular necrosis.

CLINICAL STAGES:
Stage 1 (0-24h): Nausea, vomiting, malaise — may be minimal
Stage 2 (24-72h): Right upper quadrant pain, rising transaminases
Stage 3 (72-96h): Peak hepatotoxicity — elevated AST/ALT, bilirubin, coagulopathy
Stage 4 (4 days-2 weeks): Recovery or fulminant hepatic failure

TREATMENT — RUMACK-MATTHEW NOMOGRAM:
The Rumack-Matthew nomogram guides treatment decisions based on serum acetaminophen level plotted against time since ingestion. Levels above the treatment line indicate need for N-acetylcysteine.

ANTIDOTE — N-ACETYLCYSTEINE (NAC):
N-acetylcysteine (NAC) is the antidote for acetaminophen overdose. It replenishes glutathione stores and directly detoxifies NAPQI. Most effective when administered within 8 hours of ingestion. Still beneficial up to 24 hours post-ingestion. Can be given orally (Mucomyst) or intravenously (Acetadote).
