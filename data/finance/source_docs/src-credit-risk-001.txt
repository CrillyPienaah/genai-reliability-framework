Credit Risk Management — Loan Loss Provisions and Credit Analysis

LOAN LOSS PROVISIONS (PROVISION FOR CREDIT LOSSES — PCL):
A provision for credit losses is an accounting estimate of expected future credit losses charged to the income statement. It increases the allowance for credit losses (ACL) on the balance sheet.

PCL is a forward-looking estimate — it does not mean losses have been realised. Banks increase provisions when they expect future credit deterioration based on: macroeconomic forecasts, portfolio risk migration, sector stress, or regulatory guidance.

An increase in PCL quarter-over-quarter indicates the bank expects higher future losses. This can reflect: (1) prudent conservatism in a deteriorating environment, (2) IFRS 9 stage migration from Stage 1 to Stage 2, (3) growth in the loan book, (4) a specific large credit event. Context is essential — a $500 million PCL increase on a $600 billion loan book (0.08% annualised) is very different from the same increase on a $50 billion book (1.0% annualised).

CREDIT ANALYSIS — LEVERAGE RATIOS:
Debt-to-equity (D/E) ratio = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity. Measures financial leverage.
Industry context is critical: utilities and real estate investment trusts (REITs) typically have D/E ratios of 1.5-3.0x due to capital intensity. Technology companies at 2.5x D/E would be considered highly leveraged.
Supporting metrics: interest coverage ratio (EBIT / interest expense; >3x is generally adequate), debt service coverage ratio (DSCR), net debt / EBITDA.

LLM CREDIT MEMO ACCURACY:
When an AI model generates a credit memo, all financial figures must be verified against audited financial statements. Audited financials are the authoritative source. If an LLM-generated revenue figure of $50 million contradicts audited financials showing $35 million, the LLM figure must be rejected — a 43% overstatement of revenue would materially inflate assessed repayment capacity and violate credit policy limits.
